I came across this lecture from Richard Feynman and couldn't stop
watching. It's from 1979 — recorded at the University of Auckland
as part of his Douglas Robb Memorial Lectures on quantum
electrodynamics. The video quality is modest. The content is
extraordinary.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Watch it:** [The Strange Theory of Light and Matter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rWvnxiSamPU) — Richard Feynman, Douglas Robb Memorial Lectures, 1979. About 90 minutes. Worth every second.
</Callout>

## What He's Explaining

Feynman is trying to answer a deceptively simple question: why does
light partially reflect off glass?

You've seen this. Look at a window at night — you see both through
it and a reflection of yourself at the same time. Classical physics
has a reasonable answer involving wave interference. But Feynman
throws that out and explains it the quantum way — in terms of
individual photons.

Here's the part that breaks your brain: a single photon hitting a
single piece of glass has about a 4% chance of reflecting. That's
it. No wave interference. Just probability. And nobody can tell you
which photons will reflect and which will pass through. Not even in
principle.

## The Arrow Analogy

To calculate the probability of any quantum event, Feynman uses
what he calls "arrows" — little vectors that rotate like the hand
of a stopwatch as a photon travels. The probability of an event
happening is the square of the length of the final arrow.

What makes light travel in a straight line? Not because that's the
only path it takes — photons actually explore every possible path
simultaneously. The straight line just happens to be where all the
arrows from nearby paths point in the same direction and add up.
Everywhere else they cancel out.

This is the Principle of Least Time — but explained from the bottom
up, from quantum probability rather than from classical optics.

## Why This Hit Different

I've watched a lot of science explainers. Feynman is in a different
category entirely. He's not simplifying — he's genuinely taking you
to the edge of what's known and being honest about what isn't
understood.

At one point he says something along the lines of: I'm going to
tell you how nature actually behaves, and if it seems strange and
counterintuitive, that's because it is. Don't keep asking "but why
does it do that?" The answer is: we don't know. That's the edge of
our knowledge. But here's how to calculate it.

That combination of precision and honesty is rare. Most explainers
smooth over the edges. Feynman leans into them.

## The Four Lectures

This is the second of four lectures in the series:

1. **Photons — Corpuscles of Light** — what photons are, how we
   detect them, partial reflection
2. **Fits of Reflection and Transmission** — the one above, paths
   and probabilities
3. **Electrons and their Interactions** — Feynman diagrams, how
   particles interact
4. **New Queries** — what QED means, where it breaks down, what
   comes next

All four are on YouTube. Worth your time.

## The Takeaway

Light doesn't travel in a straight line because that's the rule.
It travels in a straight line because that's where the probabilities
constructively interfere. The rule emerges from something deeper.

That's a useful way to think about a lot of things.
