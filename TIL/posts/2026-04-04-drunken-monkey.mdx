Here's a number that stops you mid-scroll: wild chimpanzees consume the equivalent of **2.5 alcoholic drinks per day**. Not from brewing, not from fermentation they engineered — just from eating fruit. Very ripe, very fermented fruit.

Researchers measured the ethanol content in 21 kinds of fruit eaten by chimps in Uganda and Ivory Coast. The fruits averaged only about 0.3% alcohol — barely anything. But chimps eat 5 to 10 percent of their body weight in fruit every single day. When you run the math and adjust for body mass, that adds up to roughly 14 grams of alcohol daily — the human equivalent of two and a half drinks.

This study adds weight to the **"drunken monkey" hypothesis** proposed by biologist Robert Dudley about 20 years ago. The idea is that humans didn't develop a taste for alcohol out of nowhere — we inherited it from primate ancestors who sought out fermented, calorie-dense fruit millions of years ago. At the time, scientists were skeptical. Now, with data like this, the picture is getting clearer. As one primatologist put it: "our relationship with alcohol goes deep back into evolutionary time, probably about 30 million years."

But the detail that really stuck with me is this: sometimes, after eating large quantities of boozy fruit, chimpanzee groups go out on patrol together — territorial excursions that carry real risk of fatal encounters with rival groups. The researchers note it's "tantalizing to think that the alcohol in the fruits might help them gather up their courage first."

Which is... extremely human. The liquid courage instinct isn't a modern social quirk. It might be wired into us at a depth we're only beginning to understand.

Somewhere in our evolutionary past, a primate reached for the fermented fruit — and apparently never really stopped.
