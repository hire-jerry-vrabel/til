## The Insight

Most job descriptions bury the real interview rubric in plain sight.
If a company tells you exactly how they define success in a role —
use it. Map every answer you give back to one of those metrics.

I'm prepping for an interview where success is defined as:

- The development team is efficient, organized, and consistently
  delivers on time
- Work is appropriately prioritized and resourced
- Quality and consistency of code across all projects
- Development and IT remain aligned to support creative production needs

That's not a job description — that's the scorecard. Here's how I'm
using it.

## Step 1 — Decode What Each Metric Is Really Saying

Every success metric is also a pain point. Companies define success
around the things that have hurt them before.

"Consistently delivers on time" — they've had a team that missed
deadlines. "Appropriately prioritized" — they've had scope creep or
poor resourcing decisions. "Quality and consistency" — they have
inconsistent code across projects. "Dev and creative aligned" — these
two teams have had friction.

Understanding the pain behind the metric helps you position your
experience as the solution.

## Step 2 — Prepare One STAR Story Per Metric

STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result.

The key is specificity. "I improved team efficiency" is forgettable.
"I introduced a sprint process adapted for agency work that included
explicit per-project capacity allocation and a formal scope triage
process — on-time delivery improved within two sprints" is memorable.

One story per metric. Four stories total. That's your interview prep.

## Step 3 — Turn Their Metrics Into Your Questions

The best interview questions come directly from the success criteria:

- "You mention consistent on-time delivery — what does the current
  estimation process look like and where do you see the biggest gaps?"
- "How do you handle prioritization when creative needs and dev
  capacity don't align?"
- "Is there a shared component library in place or is that something
  you'd want to build?"

These questions signal you read the role carefully, you understand
the problems they're trying to solve, and you're already thinking
like someone in the job.

## Step 4 — Build an Opening Statement

If they ask "why would you be successful here?" — open with a direct
reference to how they've defined success. Not generic enthusiasm.
A specific acknowledgment that you read what they wrote and it maps
to your actual experience.

## The Meta-Lesson

Most candidates prepare answers to generic interview questions.
The candidates who get offers prepare answers to THIS company's
specific definition of success.

The information is usually right there in the job description.
Most people just don't use it.
